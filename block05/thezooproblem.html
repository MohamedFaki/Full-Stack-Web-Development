Zoo's Inhabitant's Guide

General care guide for new interns to identify and care for our animals

  1. Bear

    1. Ollie

      2. As a zookeeper, it is important to provide Kodiak bears with large enclosures with plenty of space to exercise and a large water source to swim and play in. We give the bears a variety of toys, puzzle feeders, new scents, and more, to keep them mentally stimulated. A varied diet is key for Kodiak bears, and they should receive a number of different fruits, vegetables, and protein sources. Click for more detailed care

    2. Mona

      3. Polar bears need at least 1 acre of open space per bear and large, deep, non-chlorinated saltwater pools. They have low protein requirements and maintaining bears on lower protein diets is critical to support long-term kidney health. Positive reinforcement training is used to train polar bears and many other animals in several behaviors that help with medical care. Click for more detailed care

  2. Giraffes

    1. Frankie

      2. Frankie need regular hoof trims which are essential for their health. Zookeepers teach giraffes a series of small behaviors before any actual hoof care can occur. The first step is to get each giraffe to recognize a “target” and touch it with their nose, which is then reinforced until the task is mastered. Click for more detailed care

    2. Coconut

      3. Same care guide as Frankie. Do not seperate. Click for more detailed care

  3. Lions

    1. Mella

      2. Lions need a large and secure enclosure that provides them with enough space, enrichment, and shelter. They also need a balanced diet of meat and bones, as well as regular health checks and veterinary care Click for more detailed care

    2. Karl

      3. Lions need to be fed regularly with fresh meat and bones, which can be delivered through a chute or thrown by hand into their enclosure12. They also need to be monitored for their social behavior, health status, and reproductive cycles Click for more detailed care

  4. Monkeys

    1. Cookies

      2. Zoos keep these animals in groups to meet their social needs. They house groups of Howler Monkeys in large enclosures with a variety of trees, branches, vines, and ropes to climb and exercise. Zookeepers also provide the animals with a number of toys and puzzle feeders to keep them mentally stimulated. Click for more detailed care

    2. Earl

      3. Zoos keep groups of Capuchins together in large enclosures. Their enclosures contain a variety of branches, trees, ropes, and other climbing opportunities. Click for more detailed care

    3. Bananna Pudding

      4. Zoos house these primates in large enclosures where they have plenty of space to exercise, play, and socialize. They keep these creatures in groups to meet their social needs. Zookeepers also provide them with a variety of toys, puzzle feeders, and other foraging opportunities. Click for more detailed care

  5. Alligators

    1. Wren

      2. In a zoological setting the primary care component of these reptiles is a water source. It should be deep enough for the animal to submerge fully, and they should have plenty of room to grow into the enclosure. https://animals.net/american-alligator/Click here for more detailed care guide

    2. Aspen

      3. Providing food is easy, as these creatures will prey on a wide variety of species in the wild. They can be fed fish, mice, rats, rabbits, chickens, and more. Some zoological institutions also train their alligators, and other crocodilians, using positive reinforcement. One of the many things trained are stations, where the animals each move to their own spot to be fed. Click for more detailed care

    3. Mika

      4. People keep these large reptiles in both zoos and farms. For the Chinese species in particular, captive breeding programs allow researchers to maintain a population to reintroduce into the wild. Animals in zoos also help educate the public about the misconceptions associated with these reptiles. Click for more detailed care
